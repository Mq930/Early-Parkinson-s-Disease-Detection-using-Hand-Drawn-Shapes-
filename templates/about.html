About Parkinson's Disease

Understanding the condition, its symptoms, stages, and the inspiring stories of those who face it with courage

What Is Parkinson's Disease?

A Progressive Neurological Condition

Parkinson's disease is a neurodegenerative disorder that affects movement. It develops gradually, sometimes starting with a barely noticeable tremor in just one hand. The condition occurs when nerve cells (neurons) in a part of the brain called the substantia nigra become impaired or die.

These neurons produce dopamine, a chemical messenger that helps coordinate muscle movement. When dopamine production decreases, it leads to the motor symptoms associated with Parkinson's disease.

Who Does It Affect?

While Parkinson's typically affects people over 60, about 10-20% of those diagnosed are under 50. The condition affects men slightly more than women, and while age is the greatest risk factor, genetics and environmental factors may also play a role.

Symptoms & Stages

The primary movement-related symptoms of Parkinson's disease

🤝 Tremor

Often begins in one hand or finger, typically occurring at rest. The characteristic "pill-rolling" tremor between thumb and forefinger is common.

🐌 Bradykinesia

Slowness of movement that can make simple tasks difficult and time-consuming. May affect walking, writing, and other daily activities.

💪 Muscle Rigidity

Stiffness and inflexibility in the limbs and trunk, which can limit range of motion and contribute to pain and discomfort.

⚖️ Postural Instability

Impaired balance and coordination that can lead to falls. Usually develops in later stages of the disease.

🚶 Gait Changes

Shuffling walk, reduced arm swing, and difficulty starting or stopping movement. Steps may become shorter and more hesitant.

✍️ Micrographia

Handwriting becomes smaller and more cramped over time, often one of the early signs that can be detected through drawing tests.

Important symptoms that don't involve movement but significantly impact quality of life

😴 Sleep Disorders

Includes insomnia, excessive daytime sleepiness, REM sleep behavior disorder, and restless leg syndrome.

😔 Mood Changes

Depression, anxiety, and apathy are common and can occur before motor symptoms appear.

🧠 Cognitive Changes

Problems with executive function, attention, and memory. Dementia may develop in advanced stages.

👃 Loss of Smell

Reduced ability to smell (hyposmia) often occurs years before motor symptoms and affects up to 90% of patients.

🔥 Autonomic Dysfunction

Issues with blood pressure regulation, digestion, bladder control, and temperature regulation.

💊 Medication Side Effects

Dyskinesia (involuntary movements) and on-off fluctuations can develop with long-term medication use.

The progression of Parkinson's disease through five distinct stages

1

Stage 1: Mild

Symptoms are mild and don't interfere with daily activities. Tremor and movement symptoms occur on one side of the body. Changes in posture, walking, and facial expressions may be noticeable to family and friends.

2

Stage 2: Moderate

Symptoms worsen and tremor, rigidity, and movement symptoms affect both sides of the body. Walking problems and poor posture become evident. Daily tasks become more difficult and take longer to complete.

3

Stage 3: Mid-stage

Loss of balance and slowness of movement are hallmarks. Falls become more common. While still independent, symptoms significantly impair activities like dressing and eating.

4

Stage 4: Advanced

Symptoms are severe and limiting. It's possible to stand without assistance, but movement requires a walker. Daily tasks become very difficult, and living alone is often unsafe.

5

Stage 5: Advanced

Stiffness in legs makes it impossible to stand or walk. Wheelchair or bed-bound. Round-the-clock care is required. Hallucinations and delusions may occur.

Stories of Hope

Inspiring individuals who continue to make a difference while living with Parkinson's disease

Michael J. Fox

Actor & Advocate
"I am careful not to confuse excellence with perfection. Excellence, I can reach for; perfection is God's business."
Diagnosed at 29, Michael founded The Michael J. Fox Foundation, which has funded over $1 billion in Parkinson's research.

Muhammad Ali

Boxing Legend & Humanitarian
"Don't count the days, make the days count."
The boxing champion lived with Parkinson's for over 30 years, becoming a powerful symbol of courage and resilience.

Alan Alda

Actor & Science Communicator
"I'm not angry. It's just a part of life. You face it, you learn to live with it."
The M*A*S*H star continues to work in television, film, and science communication while managing his condition.
